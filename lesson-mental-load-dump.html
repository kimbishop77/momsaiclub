The Mental Load Dump

What you'll do: empty everything swirling in your head, and let Claude sort it into a plan you can actually follow.

The dentist appointment. The teacher gift. The thing you were supposed to sign. The worry you can't name. Moms carry a to-do list nobody else can see — and it's heavy. This lesson gets it out of your head.

The steps

1

Dump it all — messy is the point

Send this, then just let it pour out. No order, no punctuation rules. (Phone tip: use the little microphone on your keyboard and talk instead of type.)

COPY THIS PROMPT I'm going to dump everything on my mind. Don't fix anything yet — just listen. When I'm done, sort everything into 4 lists: To-Do This Week, Can Wait, Needs a Decision, and Just Worries.
2

Look at the "Just Worries" list

This list is quietly the best part. Half your mental load isn't tasks — it's worries pretending to be tasks. Seeing them named and separated is a real relief. For any worry that has an action hiding inside it, say: "What's one small step for this one?"

3

Turn "This Week" into a real plan

COPY THIS PROMPT Take the To-Do This Week list and fit it into my actual week. Mornings are chaos. I have pockets of time at nap time and after 8pm. Be realistic — don't schedule 12 things in one day.

Screenshot the plan or ask Claude to make it a checklist. Done.

Make it yours

  • "Which of these could my partner or kids take over? Draft the ask so it doesn't sound naggy."
  • "What on this list can I just... not do? Be honest."
  • Do it every Sunday night — it takes 10 minutes and Monday feels different.
Gentle note: this is for everyday overwhelm. If the weight feels bigger than a list — more like anxiety or depression — that deserves real support from a person, not an app. Both things can be true: use the tool, and talk to someone.

That's the lesson. Your head is not a filing cabinet. Stop asking it to be one. 💛
